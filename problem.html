Problem

The Housing Crisis Explained

San Francisco has a problem. It has some of the highest rents in the country; $3.76 per square foot, on average. People are being evicted, only half of needed housing units are being constructed each year, and it can take decades to get a structure built. How did San Francisco get here, and what can be done?

How Did We Get Here?

San Francisco has a roughly 35% home ownership rate, and 75% of the rental market is under rent control. How did we get here? Homeowners have a vested interest in keeping their property values high, and they do so via hundreds of influential political groups. Meanwhile, rent-controlled tenants care much more about eviction protections, since many of them pay rents so far below market that no reasonable amount of construction could alleviate their issues. Meanwhile, new tech products require less physical office space and benefit from greater population density, driving the expansion of Silicon Valley from its suburban strongholds to San Francisco. The solution seems simple; build more housing. But it isn't quite that easy.

Local Movements

If it was just a matter of building more housing, the problem would be easy to rectify. However, there is 50 years of history, movements, and resistance to a changing San Francisco. In 1967, residents of the Mission persuaded city government to vote down an urban restoration program out of fear of losing their neighborhood. This attitude continues. Environmental and preservationist movements caused regulation limiting the amount of commercial space that could be built in a year. It wasn't just the potentially displaced either. A wealthy couple sponsored and almost single-handedly funded a campaign to pass a new law that would require the city to have a public vote on each building over 4 stories tall.

Effects Of Byzantine Bureaucracy

Local organizations have used San Francisco's regulations to further their own influence. SF has a preliminary review that last 6 months, a chance for neighbors to appeal your permit, requires long public notice that can take years, and allows neighborhood groups to file an environmental lawsuit. Should all of these fail, locals can still put development on a citywide ballot, where everyone must vote on them. This can add years of impasse for a builder. Occasionally, this leads to morally dubious deals, where the builder donates large amounts to charity and community improvement programs. This creates many unofficial expenses that are passed on to tenants. As a result, construction costs can reach $500,000 for a 800 sq. feet unit.

Proposition 13

A former small newspaper publisher, Howard Jarvis, had an idea. He would cap property taxes at 1% of their assessed value, and prevent a rise of more than 2% per year until the property was sold again. However, landlords did not pass savings on to renters, but kept them for themselves. Prop 13 had many effects. California's property tax revenues fell by 60%. School funding, financed from property taxes, plummeted. City governments had to support more office and retail development, to boost taxes. It also caused a "lock-in" effect as property values soared, causing massive gulfs between taxes on newly sold properties and ones that had been owned for a long time. This enhanced the power of homeowners, contributing to the problem.

Effects Of The Crisis

Home prices in SF are 140% above base construction costs. This is because San Francisco has created artificial limits on housing construction. It's not just SF. Between 1955 and 1964, NYC permitted more than 11,000 units a year. Between 1980 and 1999, it permitted only 3120 per year. Manhattan real estate prices went up 284% in that time period in constant dollars. If San Francisco had a more elastic housing supply, it would make living more affordable and allow the economy to rebound thanks to increased income.
